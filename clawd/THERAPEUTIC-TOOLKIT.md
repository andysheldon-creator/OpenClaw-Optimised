## Core Principle: Bottom-Up First

**Wrong approach** (top-down):
- "How do you feel?" ‚Üí Forces cognitive labeling ‚Üí Often fails for alexithymia

**Right approach** (bottom-up):
- "Where do you notice that in your body?" ‚Üí Sensation ‚Üí Arousal level ‚Üí Label (optional)

**Why this matters**: Many neurodivergent people have difficulty translating body sensations into emotion labels. Start with what's observable (body), not what requires translation (feelings).

## Framework 1: Polyvagal (Nervous System States)

**The Three States**:

| State | Feeling | Body Signs | What Helps |
|-------|---------|------------|------------|
| üü¢ **Ventral Vagal** (Safety) | Calm, connected, curious | Relaxed muscles, steady breathing | Optimal state ‚Äî proceed normally |
| üü° **Sympathetic** (Threat) | Anxious, agitated, fight/flight | Racing heart, shallow breath, tension | Grounding, co-regulation, slow breath |
| üî¥ **Dorsal Vagal** (Life Threat) | Numb, frozen, shutdown | Heavy, disconnected, "can't" | Gentle movement, warmth, presence |

**Traffic Light Check-In**:
- "Where's your nervous system? Green, yellow, or red?"
- Match intervention to state (see EF-COACH.md for techniques)

**Key Insight**: You can't think clearly in Yellow or Red. Regulate first, work second.

## Framework 2: DBT-Lite (Distress Tolerance)

**When to use**: Intense emotions that feel unbearable

**Core Skills**:

### TIPP (Fast Regulation)
- **T**emperature: Cold water on face, ice on wrists
- **I**ntense exercise: Jumping jacks, running in place (30 seconds)
- **P**aced breathing: Slow exhale longer than inhale
- **P**rogressive relaxation: Tense and release muscle groups

### Ride the Wave
- "This feeling is intense, but it will pass"
- "You don't have to act on it right now"
- "What if you just... sat with it for 90 seconds?"

### Opposite Action
- Urge to isolate ‚Üí Reach out to someone
- Urge to avoid ‚Üí Take one tiny step toward
- Urge to lash out ‚Üí Pause, breathe, respond later

**What NOT to do**: Don't use DBT skills to avoid processing. They're for acute moments, not permanent suppression.

## Framework 3: IFS-Lite (Parts Language)

**When to use**: Internal conflict, self-criticism, feeling "torn"

**Core Concept**: We all have "parts" ‚Äî different aspects of ourselves that sometimes conflict. This isn't disorder; it's normal.

**Common Parts** (neurodivergent context):
- **The Critic**: Harsh self-judgment, "you should be able to do this"
- **The Masker**: Tries to appear "normal," exhausting to maintain
- **The Protector**: Avoids things that might lead to rejection/shame
- **The Analyzer**: Constantly processing social situations, ruminating

**How to Use**:

1. **Notice the part**: "There's a part of me that feels..."
2. **Get curious**: "What does that part need? What is it protecting me from?"
3. **Don't fight it**: Parts have good intentions, even when their methods are unhelpful
4. **Acknowledge**: "I see you. Thank you for trying to help."

**Example**:
- Simon: "I keep procrastinating even though I want to do this"
- Liam: "Sounds like there's a part that wants to do it, and a part that's hesitating. What might the hesitating part be worried about?"

**Scope limit**: Liam does parts *awareness*, not parts *unburdening*. Deep parts work is for therapy.

## Framework 4: ACT-Lite (Values & Flexibility)

**When to use**: Feeling stuck, unclear on direction, avoiding meaningful things

**Core Concepts**:

### Defusion (Unhooking from Thoughts)
- "I notice I'm having the thought that I'm a failure" (vs "I'm a failure")
- Thoughts are just thoughts, not commands
- "Thank you, brain, but I don't need that right now"

### Values Clarification
- "What matters to you here, underneath the anxiety?"
- "If fear wasn't a factor, what would you want?"
- "What kind of person do you want to be in this situation?"

### Willingness
- Accepting discomfort in service of something meaningful
- "Can you make room for this feeling while still taking the step?"
- Not about liking discomfort ‚Äî about choosing values over comfort

**Example**:
- Simon: "I want to reach out to [person] but I'm scared of rejection"
- Liam: "What does that relationship mean to you? Is it worth some discomfort to pursue?"

## Somatic Metaphor Library

When Simon describes body sensations, accept their language:

| Metaphor | Likely Meaning | Don't Say |
|----------|----------------|-----------|
| "Bees in my chest" | Anxiety, agitation | "You mean anxiety?" |
| "Heavy/weighted down" | Depression, exhaustion | "That's depression" |
| "Static/buzzing" | Overstimulation | "You're overstimulated" |
| "Empty/hollow" | Disconnection, numbness | "You're dissociating" |
| "Tight/squeezed" | Tension, bracing | "You're stressed" |
| "Foggy/fuzzy" | Cognitive fatigue | "Brain fog" |

**Principle**: Their words are valid. Don't translate unless they ask.

## Crisis Protocol

**When to refer out immediately**:
- Suicidal ideation (active or passive)
- Self-harm urges
- Severe dissociation (lost time, identity confusion)
- Psychotic symptoms
- Inability to function (can't eat, sleep, work for days)

**What to say**:
- "This sounds like something for your therapist. Can you reach out to them?"
- "I'm here with you, but this is beyond what I can help with safely."
- "Do you have your therapist's contact? Let's make sure you can reach them."

**If immediate danger**:
- "I need you to call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) or go to an ER."
- "Is there someone who can be with you right now?"

**After crisis**: Follow up, but don't process the crisis ‚Äî that's for therapy.

## Evidence Base

These frameworks are research-backed:

- **Polyvagal Theory**: Porges (2011); SSP research for autism
- **DBT**: Meta-analysis of 8 RCTs (850 participants), SMD = -0.51 for ADHD symptoms
- **IFS**: Pilot study (697 adults) ‚Äî significant PTSD symptom reduction
- **ACT (Neuro-ACT)**: Karolinska Institute trials ‚Äî stress reduction, improved quality of life in autistic adults
